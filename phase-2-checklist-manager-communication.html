COMMUNICATION STYLE CHECKLIST

Align your communication style with how modern teams actually work

This is not a “be nicer” checklist and it’s not a personality test. It’s a practical way to spot where your messages get misread, your asks misunderstood, or your tone appears sharper (or softer) than intended, especially in remote and async environments.

When to Use This Checklist

  • When your messages create confusion, rework, or long back-and-forths that should have been one decision.
  • When you’re told you’re “too blunt”, “too vague”, “too detailed”, or “hard to read” — especially in writing.
  • When remote work is amplifying tone risk: short messages feel cold; long messages feel overwhelming.
  • When you want more influence without more meetings: clearer asks, cleaner closure, stronger follow-through.
  • When you suspect your style works with some people and clashes with others (without obvious reason).

Modern Preferences Most Teams Share

  • Headline-first communication: the ask/decision/risk upfront, then the detail.
  • Skimmable formatting: short paragraphs, bullets, bolded asks, and clear next steps.
  • Async by default (when possible): fewer “quick calls”, more written clarity and documented decisions.
  • Explicit response expectations: “No rush, EOD Friday” beats silent urgency and guesswork.
  • Low-drama candour: direct, respectful, and specific — without heat, sarcasm, or hidden meaning.

Communication styles: what’s really happening

Most “communication problems” are preference mismatches, not capability issues. People aren’t reading your intent — they’re reading for risk, effort, status, and what you want them to do. The goal is not to shape-shift. It’s to package your message so it lands cleanly with the least friction.

Fast Deciders
They optimise for speed and outcomes. They skim for: recommendation, trade-offs, deadline.
  • They like: “Here’s the call. Here’s why. Here’s what I need.”
  • They hate: long preambles, “just sharing”, open-ended threads.
  • Write to them: Recommendation + 2 bullets + decision question + by-when.
Precision Seekers
They optimise for correctness and defensibility. They read for: definitions, constraints, evidence.
  • They like: clear scope, assumptions, data, “definition of done”.
  • They hate: vague asks, implied meaning, “should be fine”.
  • Write to them: Headline + context + assumptions + options + risk note.
Consensus Builders
They optimise for alignment and social safety. They read for: impact, stakeholders, buy-in.
  • They like: early heads-ups, “who needs to weigh in”, shared decisions.
  • They hate: surprise changes, decisions without consultation.
  • Write to them: Headline + impact + who’s involved + “any objections by…”.
Warmth & Trust Optimisers
They optimise for relationship quality. They read for: tone, respect, care (especially in async).
  • They like: acknowledgement, clarity without sharp edges, collaborative language.
  • They hate: cold directives, public corrections, clipped messages under pressure.
  • Write to them: 1 warm line + clear ask + options + “happy to adjust”.
Preference probe (use once, then adapt)
Quick check so I communicate in your preferred way:
Do you prefer short headlines or more context?
For decisions, do you want a recommendation or options?
And should I use chat, email, or a doc link for anything with moving parts?
Power + Psychology
  • Senior stakeholders often read for risk and accountability: include “Decision needed”, “Risk”, “Owner”, “Date”.
  • Status-sensitive people react badly to public correction: move critique private, keep public messages neutral.
  • Anxious / overloaded people need certainty and low cognitive load: fewer choices, clearer next step, explicit timelines.
  • Defensive people scan for blame: separate facts from interpretation (“Here’s what happened” vs “You did…”).

Your Communication Style Checklist

I lead with the headline: the ask, decision needed, or key point — before background or context.
I state the “by when” and “by who” clearly, so urgency and ownership are not implied or guessed.
My messages are skimmable: short paragraphs, bullets, and one clear next step rather than a wall of text.
I choose the right channel (async message, email, call, doc) based on risk, complexity and sensitivity — not habit.
I make assumptions visible (“I’m assuming X — correct me if wrong”) to prevent silent misalignment.
My written tone lands as calm and respectful, even when I’m under pressure or chasing outcomes.
I avoid ambiguity that triggers misreads (sarcasm, vague praise, “as discussed…”, passive-aggressive phrasing).
I adapt for accessibility and neurodiversity: clear structure, fewer hidden cues, and lower cognitive load.
I can say “no” or “not now” cleanly, with a reason and an alternative — without over-explaining or apologising excessively.
I raise risks early and neutrally, without sounding alarmist or making it personal.
When there’s tension, I use curiosity and facts (not labels) to move the conversation forward.
I set and honour response-time expectations (e.g. “I’ll reply by tomorrow”, “No rush until Friday”).
I close loops in writing: decisions, owners, and next steps are captured so nothing relies on memory.
On remote calls, I summarise out loud and then confirm in chat or a note so alignment survives the meeting.
