FORMAL PROCESS GUIDE

Know what's coming. Navigate capability, performance, grievance and disciplinary processes with calm, credible control

This is a practical, UK workplace guide to help you understand what each process is, what “fair” usually looks like, and how to protect yourself through clear evidence, clean notes, smart questions and follow-up in writing. If you’re in a high-stakes situation (dismissal risk, discrimination, whistleblowing, protected conversations, settlement offers), consider advice from your union/rep or a qualified employment professional.

When to Use This Guide

  • You’ve been invited to a meeting and the wording feels formal (“investigation”, “hearing”, “capability”, “performance”, “grievance”).
  • You want to know what the employer should do vs what’s optional.
  • You want to avoid being ambushed, rushed, or pressured into agreeing vague actions.
  • You need a clean plan for notes, evidence, and follow-up emails.
  • You want to keep your position work-defensible if this escalates later.

Core Principles (Plain English)

Process clarity Evidence & specifics Right to respond
  • Process is power. When you know the steps, you stop reacting and start steering.
  • Facts beat feelings. Keep everything anchored to dates, examples, outputs and impact.
  • Specifics or it didn’t happen. “Concerns” without examples are not something you can reasonably answer.
  • Slow the tempo. You can request time to review evidence, take notes, and respond properly.
  • Write it down. Notes + recap emails reduce “he said / she said” and stop narrative drift.

Modern insight: process gets misused more than people admit

Formal processes are meant to be fair. But in the real world, they can be used to create a paper trail, shift blame, or force an exit. Your goal is simple: clarity, evidence, and a controlled record.

“Investigations” are narrative battles
The first version of events often becomes the “truth”. You protect yourself by capturing facts, timelines, and requests early.
Tempo control is leverage
Pressure tactics thrive on speed. Asking for evidence in advance, confirming the purpose of the meeting, and taking notes reduces manipulation.
“In writing” is not rude — it’s professional
A calm recap email is the cleanest way to stop revisionism: what was discussed, what was agreed, what happens next.

Quick discriminator

Grievance = your complaint about treatment/decision.
Performance = outputs/standards not met (often targets/quality).
Capability = ability to do the job (skills or health/illness impacts).

The professional trapdoor

Quick Process Timelines (at a glance)
These are “typical” shapes. Your employer’s policy may vary. Use this to spot missing steps and ask the right questions.
Process Typical stages Your leverage points
Disciplinary Investigation → invite to hearing (allegations + evidence) → hearing → outcome (warning/dismissal) → appeal Request evidence in advance; challenge vagueness; bring companion; take notes; ask for reasons + appeal route in writing
Grievance Raise grievance (written) → investigation/fact-find → meeting → outcome → appeal Define the outcome you want; keep it factual; provide timeline + evidence; ask for scope, findings, and actions

Quick Coverage Checklist

Final audit: this is about clarity, evidence, and controlled documentation — not emotional debate.
I know which process this is (disciplinary / grievance / performance / capability).
I know the stage (informal / investigation / hearing / outcome / appeal).
I can state the headline issue in one sentence, without emotion or blame language.
I’ve defined my objective (what outcome I’m seeking).
I have asked for evidence/examples (or I have them already).
I know whether I’m bringing a companion/rep (or I’ve asked if I can).
I have key facts ready (dates, outputs, context) and I can share them cleanly.
I have identified what feels unfair/risky (vagueness, bias, no chance to respond).
I have an opening line, a key question, and a boundary line prepared.
I have a follow-up plan: recap email + what I want confirmed in writing.
If adjustments/health are relevant, I’m prepared to request reasonable adjustments support.
If an outcome is unfair, I understand the idea of appeal grounds (procedure/reasonableness/new evidence).
