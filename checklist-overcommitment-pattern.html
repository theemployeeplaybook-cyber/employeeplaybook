OVERCOMMITMENT PATTERN CHECKLIST

Spot and stop the patterns that lead to burnout

Identify where you're overcommitting, understand why it happens, and create sustainable boundaries. Recognize the warning signs before exhaustion sets in and build a realistic, maintainable workload.

When to Use This Checklist

  • When you consistently work late or feel exhausted despite "normal" hours.
  • When you say "yes" to requests automatically, then regret it later.
  • When your to-do list keeps growing but nothing ever gets removed.
  • When you feel guilty for taking breaks or time off.
  • When feedback suggests you're taking on too much or struggling to delegate.
  • When you're covering for others regularly without reciprocal support.

Pro Tips

  • Complete this checklist when you're calm, not in the middle of a crisis.
  • Be brutally honest—overcommitment patterns thrive on self-deception.
  • Track your "yeses" for a week before completing this checklist.
  • Notice which items trigger defensiveness—those are likely your core patterns.
  • Share your results with a trusted colleague or mentor for perspective.
  • Revisit this checklist quarterly to track progress on sustainable changes.

Your Overcommitment Pattern Checklist

I have clear working hours and consistently finish within them (except for rare, planned exceptions).
I delegate appropriate tasks to others rather than doing everything myself to ensure it's "done right."
I can comfortably say "no" or "not now" to requests that don't align with my priorities.
My calendar reflects my actual capacity, with buffer time between meetings and realistic task estimates.
I take proper lunch breaks and disconnect completely during time off/vacation.
I recognize when I'm taking on emotional labor or work that should be shared across the team.
I proactively communicate capacity constraints before they become crises.
I regularly review and drop low-value tasks when higher priorities emerge.
I can distinguish between my actual responsibilities and self-imposed "shoulds."
I estimate tasks accurately, accounting for interruptions and unexpected work.
I trust others to complete delegated work without micromanaging their process.
I have a clear system for prioriting work that my manager agrees with.
