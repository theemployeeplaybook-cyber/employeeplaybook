RECOVERY & RESILIENCE GUIDE

Recover without collapsing. Rebuild capacity, protect your nervous system, and return with leverage

This is a work-safe recovery plan for when you’ve been running hot for too long — after overload, conflict, a high-pressure quarter, performance stress, or sustained uncertainty. You’ll build a 72-hour stabilisation plan, a 7-day reset, and a 2–4 week resilience ladder with guardrails that stop the next crash.

When to Use This Guide

  • You’re functioning, but it feels like you’re doing life on “battery saver mode”.
  • You can’t switch off — your brain keeps scanning for problems even when nothing is happening.
  • You’re more reactive than usual: snappy, tearful, or numb.
  • You took time off… and came back still tired (or more anxious).
  • You want a plan that protects your health and your reputation at the same time.

How Recovery Actually Works

Stabilise first Guardrails next Rebuild last
  • Recovery isn’t “rest”. It’s restoring baseline: sleep depth, energy stability, and cognitive clarity.
  • Micro-recovery beats weekend recovery. Ten minutes daily outperforms one big “crash day”.
  • Guardrails beat motivation. If you return to the same triggers, you repeat the same pattern.
  • Build capacity like a ladder. Stabilise → small wins → sustained habits → wider bandwidth.
  • Make it work-defensible. Your plan should be safe if read later: facts, impact, requests, and boundaries.

Modern insight: why you can’t “just bounce back”

Burnout is rarely one big event. It’s accumulated load + lack of recovery cycles + weak boundaries. We fix the system — not your personality.

Load is cumulative
Your nervous system doesn’t only respond to “big problems”. It responds to constant context switching, ambiguity, vigilance, and social pressure — all day, every day.
The recovery paradox
When you’re depleted, rest can feel unsafe — like you’re “falling behind”. That’s why we start with stabilisation and guardrails, not “self-care theatre”.
Small cycles rebuild bandwidth
Resilience isn’t toughness. It’s repeatable recovery. The goal: regain choice — calm thinking, better boundaries, and fewer emotional spikes.
