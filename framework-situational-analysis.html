SITUATIONAL ANALYSIS FRAMEWORK

Turn a messy situation into a clear brief and a defensible next move

This tool helps you separate facts from noise, map power and constraints, and produce a sharp output: what’s really happening, what matters, and the safest step that increases control.
Built for high-stakes workplace moments. It supports structured thinking and documentation — it isn’t legal advice. If you feel unsafe, prioritise safety and appropriate support.

When to Use This Framework

  • When you’re getting conflicting instructions and you need a clean “what’s true / what’s not” view.
  • When politics or power dynamics are distorting what people say vs what they do.
  • When you’re being judged (performance narrative risk) and need a defensible record and plan.
  • Before a difficult conversation — so your message is outcome-led and hard to dismiss.
  • When you need to choose a move that protects your energy, credibility, and position.

Pro Tips (the rules that actually win)

  • Decisions improve fast when you force: Objective → Constraints → Options → Trade-offs → Next move.
  • Power is practical: who can approve, who can block, who controls the narrative.
  • Write like a witness: events + dates + outputs, not motives and labels.
  • Pick a no-regrets move first (reduces exposure, increases clarity) — then iterate.
  • If your plan can’t survive being forwarded, it isn’t safe enough yet.

What works reliably

  • Make risk legible: impact × likelihood, then reduce exposure (written recaps, clarity, fewer private pressure points).
  • Ask for a decision: “Which of these trade-offs do you want?” beats “this is too much”.
  • Control the record: if it matters, it should exist in writing.

Common pitfalls

  • Explaining instead of deciding: long narratives that never land a decision.
  • Arguing motives: invites debate; stick to behaviour, impact, and process.
  • Taking ownership without authority: you become the blame sponge for someone else’s decision.

Power move (clean)

Protection lens

Pitfalls to avoid

  • Open-ended asks: “can we align?” is too soft. Ask for a decision/owner/trade-off.
  • Over-explaining: stick to 3–5 proof points; your credibility rises when you’re concise.
  • Private agreement: if it matters, recap it in writing.

Quick Coverage Checklist

This checklist catches the failure points that create exposure: unclear ownership, missing proof, fuzzy asks, and plans that rely on goodwill.
My summary is factual (events, dates, outputs) — not motives, labels, or therapy language.
I can state the objective and what success looks like in observable terms.
I’ve named the constraints and my non-negotiables (red lines).
I know who owns the decision and who shapes the narrative.
I have 3–5 proof points and/or a timeline that supports my position.
I have three options with trade-offs (comply / trade-off / control).
I’ve chosen a no-regrets first move that reduces exposure and increases clarity.
My ask is specific (owner/decision/trade-off) and my language is forwardable.
