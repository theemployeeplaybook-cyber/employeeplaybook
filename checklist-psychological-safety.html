PSYCHOLOGICAL SAFETY CHECKLIST

Assess whether your team is truly safe to speak up to, and what to do if it isn’t

Psychological safety is not “nice culture”. It’s whether you can raise risks, disagree, ask for help, and admit mistakes without social punishment. This checklist gives you a clean read on what’s real in your environment — and the smartest next move based on your score.

When to Use This Checklist

  • When you’re editing yourself in meetings — and you can’t tell if it’s “strategy” or fear.
  • When mistakes feel expensive, even small ones (blame, shaming, “gotcha” energy).
  • When the team looks calm on the surface, but nobody disagrees or raises risks.
  • When you suspect cliques, favourites, quiet retaliation, or rule-by-mood leadership.
  • When you’re deciding whether to lean in, protect your position, escalate, or exit.

Pro Tips

  • Score it based on observed patterns, not “intent” or “values posters”.
  • One big “yes” (e.g., friendly manager) doesn’t cancel multiple quiet “no” signals (e.g., idea theft, ridicule, retaliation).
  • Start with micro-tests: raise something small and watch the system response.
  • If safety is low, prioritise evidence, allies, and clean documentation — not emotional persuasion.
  • Use your score to choose a strategy: build, stabilise, or protect and plan.

Your Psychological Safety Checklist

I can raise a risk or concern without being labelled “negative”, “difficult”, or “not a team player”.
Mistakes are treated as learning (what happened, what we change) rather than blame (who to punish).
Disagreement is allowed in meetings — respectful challenge is not punished later.
The team avoids undermining behaviours (interrupting, eye-rolling, sarcasm, public shaming, “jokes” at someone’s expense).
Credit and ownership are handled fairly (no idea-theft, no rewriting history, no favourites always “winning”).
Expectations and “rules” are consistent — outcomes are not decided by mood, politics, or who said it.
My manager responds predictably to feedback and bad news — the reaction is about the issue, not my character.
Feedback flows both ways and stays specific — it isn’t vague criticism, humiliation, or personal attacks.
When someone is harmed or a boundary is crossed, the team repairs it (acknowledges, learns, adjusts) instead of pretending it didn’t happen.
Decisions and priorities are explained clearly (what we’re optimising for, what trade-offs we accept).
Remote or hybrid work doesn’t create a second-class experience (equal airtime, no “side room” decisions, no exclusion by default).
If psychological safety drops, I know the safest next step: what to document, who to speak to, and how to frame it.
